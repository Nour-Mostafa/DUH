Atom

It emits tremendous energies that illuminate space. Despite its small size, the atom carries T

The origin of the atom and its formation in space


After the Big Bang occurred, the temperature of the universe began to gradually decrease, allowing the basic components of matter to form.
At the beginning of cooling, elementary particles such as quarts and fluons were formed, and then merged to form protons and tetrons.
About 350,000 years after the Big Bang, the first atom was formed when electrons bonded with protons and neutrons to form mainly hydrogen and helium, the two elements that form the basis of the universe.

Scientists' belief about the origin of the universe and the composition of matter

In ancient times and up until the beginning of modern times, scientists believed that the universe was made up of four basic elements: earth, water, air, and fire. With the advancement of science and the emergence of modern chemistry, scientists began to understand that chemical elements, such as hydrogen, oxygen, and carbon, are the true components of matter. With the development of astrophysics, it became clear that the universe is composed primarily of hydrogen (about 75%) and helium (about 24%), with a small percentage of other elements (that were formed in stars as a result of nuclear fusion reactions).

Discovery of the atom and scientists' understanding of its structure

At the beginning of the discovery of the atom in ancient times, Greek philosophers such as Democritus proposed the concept of the atom as an indivisible body, but this was just a philosophical idea without scientific evidence. After the passage of time and the emergence and development of science in the 19th century, scientists began to present experimental evidence confirming the existence of the atom, such as Joseph Proust's law of definite proportions and John Walton's atomic theory, which suggested that materials are composed of small, specific atoms. The atomic model was gradually developing until the emergence of the new atomic model, where Joseph Thomson proposed the "positive sphere" model of the atom, then Ernest Rutherford discovered the positive nucleus central to the atom. Finally, Nielsboer developed the atomic model with energy levels for electrons

Basic components of the universe

After years of research and study, scientists have concluded that the universe consists of several main types of matter and energy, distributed as follows:
1. Dark energy: dark energy
2. Dark matter: dark matter
3. Baryonic matter: baryonic matter.

Basic components of the universe

Dark energy: >It makes up about 68% of the universe. This dark energy is a mysterious force responsible for the acceleration of the expansion of the universe. Although not fully understood, dark energy is thought to pervade the universe and push galaxies apart.





Dark matter: >It makes up about 27% of the universe. Dark matter is an invisible substance that does not interact, so it cannot be seen directly, but its gravitational effects are clear, as it works to bind galaxies together and maintain the stability of cosmic structures. Dark matter is believed to contain fundamental particles that have not yet been discovered.





Barynic matter: The visible natural matter: It only made up about 5% of the universe. This is the matter that we know and deal with every day, and it includes everything from stars, planets, and cosmic dust to living organisms.

Current research challenges on the components of the universe

Dark energy and dark matter are still major mysteries, and scientists do not know much about the nature of these materials. Work is currently underway to develop theories and scientific tools to try to monitor and understand these materials better, as well as to develop more accurate telescopes and probes to monitor the universe and interpret its signals, including future projects to monitor the cosmic rumours in the very early stages.

Summary

The universe is made up of a complex mixture of dark energy, dark matter, and normal matter. Water relies on advanced techniques and sophisticated mathematical models to understand how these components contribute to the formation, structure, and evolution of the universe.
